Everyone tells you the same thing about a new product: _remove_ friction from signup, don't add it. Open the doors, let anyone in, worry about scale later. I went the other way on purpose. I want TheTaleTribe to feel a little exclusive, so instead of a signup form you get an invite system — you actually _apply_ to become an author. And at least for now, I'm not approving everyone who applies. Before I open the gates to the world, I want to seed the platform with a few real stories and partner with some actual authors, so that whoever shows up next walks into something that already feels alive instead of empty. That's the romantic reason. There's also a colder, more practical one.

## Every Signup Is a Promise

Every account that signs up gets a real, working experience on day one — the full set of AI writing tools, not some stripped-down trial. That first impression matters to me, and an open door makes it impossible to protect: I'd have no idea how many people were arriving on a given morning, or whether everything would hold up well enough to give each of them a good run at it. Keeping the pace deliberate means everyone who gets in walks into something I've actually made sure is ready for them.

It really comes down to control. Instead of throwing the doors open and reacting to whatever comes through, I get to set the pace myself — one approved writer at a time — and grow only as fast as I can keep the experience genuinely good.

## How the Gate Actually Works

The mechanism itself is deliberately unglamorous. If you want in, you don't get a signup form that drops you straight into an account — you leave your email, and that quietly files a _pending request_ behind the scenes. I look at those requests and approve them by hand.

The moment I do, an automated step takes over: it generates a one-time **magic sign-in link**, emails it to you (with a genuinely too-excited "You're in!" subject line), and _that_ link is what actually creates your account when you click it. There's no password to set and no way to self-serve past the queue — the only door in runs through a request I've personally waved through. It's about as low-tech as gatekeeping gets, but it means the growth rate has exactly one knob, and I'm the one holding it.

## The Part That Gets to Me

I won't pretend the slow growth doesn't get to me sometimes. There's a very particular feeling as a solo builder watching a signup number tick up by ones and twos instead of the hockey stick you see in every founder story you've ever read. I'd be lying if I said I didn't occasionally wonder whether I was just being overly cautious, holding my own project back out of fear.

But the more time passes, the more I think slow was actually the _right_ call, not just the safe one. The people who do get in tend to actually use the thing — write real chapters, come back the next week — rather than bouncing off after one curious poke. It's easier to notice when something's broken, or when a feature nobody asked for is sitting there unused, when you can practically recognize your own users by name. And honestly, at this size I can still keep a real feel for how the whole thing is behaving — I'm nowhere near the point where the platform feels stretched, so the growth has stayed steady and manageable the whole way through rather than something I've had to white-knuckle.

## Opening the Doors, Eventually

At some point I do want to open up properly. But I'd rather do that once I've seen the whole thing actually hold up under real usage — the quotas, the guardrails, the way it behaves under load — rather than betting the experience on a guess. Slow, deliberate growth isn't the exciting version of this story, but it's the version where I actually get to see how the app holds up before betting the whole thing on finding out the hard way.
